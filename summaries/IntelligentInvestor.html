The Intelligent Investor

Chapter 1

The purpose of this book is to introduce the reader to the concept of the intelligent investor. The phrase “reckless investors” was once used by the Wall Street Journal to describe those who were reckless with their money. The defensive investor is one who is interested in safety and freedom from bother. The distinction between investment and speculation in common stocks has always been a useful one and its disappearance is a cause for concern. We must prevent our readers from accepting the common jargon which applies the term “investor” to anybody and everybody in the stock market. We trust that the reader of this book will gain a reasonably clear idea of the risks that are inherent in common-stock commitments. In 1965 the investor could obtain about 4½% on high-grade taxable bonds and 3¼% on good tax-free bonds. The dividend return on leading common stocks (with the DJIA at 892) was only about 3.2%. This fact, and others, suggested caution. Few people were willing to consider the possibility that the high rate of advance in stock prices are too high. The major change since 1964 has been the rise in interest rates on first-grade bonds to record high levels. The obtainable return on good corporate issues is now about 7½% and even more against 4½% in 1964. The dividend return on DJIA-type stocks had a fair advance also during the market decline of 1969–70. The interest and principal payments on good bonds are much better protected and therefore more certain than the dividends and price appreciation on stocks. We cannot be certain that bonds will work out better than stocks from today’s levels. There is the possibility—though we consider it remote—of an accelerating inflation, which in one way or another would have to make stock equities preferable to bonds. The defensive investor must confine himself to the shares of important companies with a long record of profitable operations and in strong financial condition. Aggressive investors may buy other types of common stocks, but they should be on a definitely attractive basis as established by intelligent analysis. The third is the device of “dollar-cost averaging,” which means simply that the practitioner invests in common stocks the same number of dollars each month or each quarter. The author says it is possible for an investor to benefit greatly by making correct predictions when Wall Street as a whole is making incorrect ones. But that is only theoretical. How many enterprising investors could count on having the acumen or prophetic gift to beat the professional analysts at their favorite game, he asks. Investing is a unique kind of casino—one where you cannot lose in the end. Graham urges you to invest only if you would be comfortable owning a stock even if you had no way of knowing its daily share price. By speculating instead of investing, you lower your own odds of building wealth and raise someone else’s. In the 1990s, America became the Speculation Nation, populated with traders who went shooting from stock to stock like grasshoppers whizzing around in an August hay field. To reach your long-term financial goals, you must be sustainably and reliably right. In short streaks, so long as your luck holds out, they will get you killed. By 1999 at least six million people were trading online. Barbra Streisand: “I’m Taurus the bull, so I react to red. If I see red, I sell my stocks quickly” Juno Online Services unveiled a trailblazing business plan: to lose as much money as possible. Many investors sell their crummiest stocks late in the year to lock in losses that can cut their tax bills. Professional money managers grow more cautious as the year draws to a close. The Foolish Four was one of the most cockamamie stock-picking formulas ever concocted. In 2000 alone, the four Foolish stocks lost 14% while the Dow dropped by just 4.7%. Never mingle the money in your account with what’s in your investment accounts. Never allow your thinking to spill over into your investing activities. Don’t confuse investment with investment.

Chapter 2

Inflation, and the fight against it, has been very much in the public’s mind in recent years. The shrinkage in the purchasing power of the dollar in the past, and particularly the fear (or hope by speculators) of a serious further decline in the future, has greatly influenced the thinking of Wall Street. Common stocks have indeed done better than bonds over a long period of time in the past. There is no close time connection between inflationary (or deflationary) conditions and the movement of common-stock earnings and prices. Both stock earnings and stock prices as a whole have declined since 1965. The investor cannot count on much above the recent five-year rate earned. In the economic cycles of the past, good business was accompanied by a rising price level and poor business by falling prices. It was generally felt that “a little inflation’ was helpful to business profits. But the figures in Table 2-2 indicate that so far from inflation having benefited our corporations and their shareholders, its effect has been the opposite. Inflation in the U.S. has probably run around 1% annually for the past 20 years. Money Illusion: What psychologists call the “money illusion.” If you receive a 2% raise when inflation runs at 4%, you will almost certainly feel better than when it runs at 2% in a year. As recently as 1973–1982, the U.S. went through one of the most painful bursts of inflation in our history. 69% of the world’s market-oriented countries have suffered at least one year in which inflation ran at an annualized rate of 25% or more. On average, those inflationary periods destroyed 53% of an investor’s purchasing power. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities are U.S. government bonds that automatically go up in value when inflation rises. When the value of your TIPS bond rises as inflation heats up, the Internal Revenue Service regards that increase in value as taxable income. Because of this exasperating tax complication, TIPS are best suited for a tax-deferred retirement account.

Chapter 4

We have suggested as a fundamental guiding rule that the investor should never have less than 25% or more than 75% of his funds in common stocks. The average investor has several choices among high-grade bonds. Those in high income-tax brackets can undoubtedly obtain a better net yield from good tax-free issues than from taxable ones. The yield on these bonds will vary both with the quality and the maturity, with the shorter maturities giving the lower return. An offering of New Housing issues in July 1971 yielded as high as 5.8%, free from both Federal and state taxes. Both obligations have the “full faith and credit” of the United States government behind them. The bond form and the preferred-stock form, as hitherto discussed, are well-understood and relatively simple matters. The best-known types are convertible and similar issues, and income bonds. In the latter type, interest does not have to be paid unless it is earned by the company. In strict logic, all investment-grade preferred stocks should be bought by corporations. The most striking thing about Graham’s discussion of how to allocate your assets between stocks and bonds is that he never mentions the word “age.” That sets his advice firmly against the winds of conventional wisdom. An 89-year-old with $3 million, an ample pension, and a gaggle of grandchildren would be foolish to move most of her money into bonds. Graham advises you never to have more than 75% of your total assets in stocks. For a tiny minority of investors, a 100%-stock portfolio may make sense. If not, steer mostly clear of stocks, edging toward Graham’s maximum of 75% in bonds or cash. Bond funds offer cheap and easy diversification, plus monthly income. Major firms like Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab, and T. Rowe Price offer a broad menu of bond funds. For investors who can confidently leave some cash untouched for years to come, inflation-protected “I-bonds” recently offered an attractive yield of around 4%. In most cases, the high expenses of owning an annuity will overwhelm its advantages. Preferred shares are less secure than bonds, since they have only a secondary claim on a company’s assets if it goes bankrupt. No intelligent investor would ever buy a stock for its dividend income alone.
